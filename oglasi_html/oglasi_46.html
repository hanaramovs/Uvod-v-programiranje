This singular house in Besançon, dating from 1883, was indeed imagined and created by the famous painter Emile Isenbart. From its views over the Doubs and its lush park, he drew, throughout his career, true sources of inspiration for many of his works. Built in stone, its spaces were designed by and for the painter, creating a uniqueness in its layouts. The entrance opens onto a hall featuring one of the house's centerpiece: its solid wood staircase marked by stained glass panels. The ground floor includes the living space. To the left, an office has access through glass doors to the former studio of the painter. Here, the volumes are remarkable. A ceiling height of more than 4m is emphasized by its original French ceiling. Facing it, the dining room is adorned with stained glass windows in bright colors and several works painted on glued canvases that depict the fables of La Fontaine. Continuing on, the kitchen space is completely to be redesigned. On the first floor, a bathroom area to be rethought and several bedrooms. On a half level, the second artist's studio extends above the first. The northern light and the 4m height again testify to a use conducive to creation. Accessible via a mezzanine, a bedroom enjoys a beautiful view of the Doubs. The top level consists of former maid's rooms as well as an attic that can be developed with about 140 m2 of usable space. The slate roof is recent and in perfect condition. The floors, the painter's frescoes, the French ceiling, and the many stained glass windows still present contribute to the undeniable historical character of this house. The land includes two terraces, a meadow, and a wood. The house is built on a cellar and has a garage that opens onto the street. The architectural line and the numerous character elements will allow new arrivals—families, collectors, musicians, dancers—to unleash their imagination to modernize its spaces while preserving the spirit of the place.
